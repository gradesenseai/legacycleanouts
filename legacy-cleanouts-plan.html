The wedge in one paragraph

Springfield has five verified local junk-removal operators and one senior move manager. Zero are positioned for the inheritance / downsizing segment. The strongest "established" competitor is Easy Cleanouts (LLC filed July 2021, 4–5 years old, stock-photo testimonials). Your differentiation is real and uncopyable: a father-son family operator who sorts respectfully, separates family papers, gets donation receipts, and quotes a flat fee on the phone — while privately retaining all resale upside as a second profit center.

Business model in plain terms

Profit Center 1 — The Haul (visible): Customer pays $499 / $799 / $1,299 flat. Net haul margin 60–75%.

Profit Center 2 — Private Resale (the upside): Items found during cleanouts get listed/flipped on your timeline. Average $100–$300 net per job. Hot inheritance jobs: $500–$1,500+. Customer has no claim — title transferred at pickup.

Niche & Customer Segments

Why this niche works

You're not selling commodity junk removal. The customer isn't comparison-shopping you against Waste Walkers Dispatch. They're solving a specific, emotionally-loaded problem (Grandpa's house, downsizing, a property left half-cleared by previous owners) where speed, dignity, and certainty are worth more than a $50 price difference. Pricing in Springfield is opaque — 4 of 5 competitors are quote-only — and no operator owns this segment.

Three target customers (in priority order)

Priority 1

The Out-of-Town Heir

40–60 years old, lives 2+ hours away. Just inherited a parent's or grandparent's house in Springfield or 60-mile radius. House is full of 30–50 years of stuff. Wants the house emptied, the good stuff dealt with, and to fly home. Price-insensitive in the moment. Highest LTV — their realtor often becomes a repeat referral source for your next probate cleanout.

Priority 2

The Local Downsizer

60–75 years old, Springfield/Ozarks resident. Moving to smaller home, retirement community, or assisted living. Adult kids pressuring them to clear the back shed, workshop, or basement. Often has a forgotten back shop with 15+ years of tools and hobby supplies. Donation receipts and family-photo separation are emotional unlocks.

Priority 3

The Realtor's Problem Property

Referral channel, not direct. Vacant property listed for sale. Previous owner moved out, took what they wanted, left the rest. Realtor needs it photo-ready in 5–10 days. 3–5 active realtor relationships can supply meaningful recurring volume.

On-job 5-step workflow

  1. Walk-through with customer (10 min). Confirm scope. Identify items they want kept. Separate "family photos & papers" into a labeled box.
  2. Quick sort triage. Three piles: keep-back (customer's), donate (Habitat ReStore, Salvation Army), haul (truck). Privately note resale-worthy items as you go — don't announce them.
  3. Haul. Donation items, then trash, then resale items kept separate in totes.
  4. Sweep clean & sign-off. Walk the space with the customer. Payment on phone via Square/Stripe.
  5. Within 48 hours: Drop donations and get receipts. Stage and list resale items.

Capacity rule: When your garage is full, stop pulling resale items from new jobs and let them go to dump. The model self-regulates. By month 6, consider hiring a church teen to handle listings/photos for an hourly wage or commission — buys back your weekends.

Disposal & restricted items

Noble Hill Sanitary Landfill is your main dump (commercial rate $41.77/ton, $20 minimum). Restricted items (tires, electronics, freon appliances, paint, batteries) are NOT accepted at Noble Hill — build a $50–$150 add-on per category. Don't eat the cost.

The contract clause that protects you

Every invoice must include the following sentence. It bulletproofs you against any future "they sold my mom's stuff" complaint.

Title-Transfer Clause (paste into every invoice)
Upon completion of service and payment, all items removed from the property become the property of Legacy Cleanouts LLC, which retains full rights to dispose, donate, recycle, or resell at its discretion. Customer waives any claim to value of removed items. Customer is responsible for separating any items they wish to retain prior to service.

Insurance, licensing, structure

  • LLC: File with Missouri Secretary of State, $50, do this week 1
  • EIN: Free, 5 minutes online with IRS
  • Springfield business license: Same-day, ~$30
  • General liability ($1M): $500–$1,200/yr through Hiscox / Thimble / local broker
  • Commercial auto: $1,500–$2,500/yr — required the moment you haul for pay. Personal auto will NOT cover.
  • Workers' comp: Not required while using 1099 contractors (avoid W-2 until year 2)

Labor model

Your son: Helper for sorting, light items, customer charm. No lifting, no ladders, no tools. Strict.
Church teen/college contractors: $15–$20/hr as 1099, hire per-job. Build a bench of 3–4 people. First to confirm gets the work. Have them sign a basic 1099 contractor agreement.

Risks & Mitigations

Demand risk — the ad test fails
Lead with the niche framing in copy, not generic junk hauling. Two creative variants. If still cold after 5 days, the niche framing or pricing is off — drop Sweep tier to $399 and rerun one cycle before pivoting. Mitigation: bootstrap path delays paid ads to job #2, so demand validates on warm leads first.
Pricing risk — $499 is too high for your market
Kill criterion: lead-to-book under 20% across 8+ leads. Drop tier-1 by $100 and retest. Conversely, if booking rate exceeds 40%, raise tiers by $100 within 30 days. Mitigation: the niche allows premium pricing because customers aren't comparison-shopping.
Operational risk — hot lead, no truck
Have same-day dump trailer rental relationship pre-established (Junk It Roll Offs, Dumpster 417). Build $75 rental cost into Cleanout/Legacy tier jobs as temp line item until you own equipment. Mitigation: never take a job you can't fulfill — reschedule politely with a discount; angry customer is worse than a one-week delay.
"My stuff is valuable" customer dispute
The filter sentence in the phone script + the title-transfer clause in every invoice. Refer would-be problem customers to estate sale companies instead. Mitigation: don't take the wrong-fit customer — refer to Smooth Transitions of Southwest Missouri or local auction houses.
Insurance risk — someone hurt on a job
Your son does NO lifting, ladders, or tools. Teens sign 1099 contractor agreements. GL covers third-party claims. Commercial auto is non-negotiable. Mitigation: bootstrap path uses Thimble per-job until you can afford annual policies.
Labor risk — church teen pipeline flakes
Maintain a bench of 3–4 1099 contractors. Text-blast jobs to all; first to confirm wins. Mitigation: don't depend on any single helper.
Reputation risk — customer realizes you sold something
Title-transfer clause in contract. Filter sentence in calls. Don't actively lie if asked. Mitigation: bulk-out generic items quickly so individual flips aren't visible to local customers. Use Branson auction houses instead of Springfield ones for higher-profile items.
Competitive copycat — Easy Cleanouts launches a "legacy" line
They've had 4–5 years to do this and haven't. By the time they'd copy you, you'll have brand equity, channel relationships, and the actual family-run story they can only fake. Mitigation: speed and authenticity beat copy.
Capital risk — first job falls through, no cash for insurance
Bootstrap path stages costs against revenue. If first booked job cancels, you're out $110 in setup costs but still have the LLC, license, and infrastructure. Mitigation: book 2 warm leads in parallel, not just one — first one funds insurance, second one funds the next phase.
